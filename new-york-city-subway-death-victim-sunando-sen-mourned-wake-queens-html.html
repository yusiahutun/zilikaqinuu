New York City subway death: Victim Sunando Sen mourned during wake in Queens

New York City subway-push victim mourned during intimate and heartrending wake in Queens, remembered for 'quiet strength'

  • The remains of Sundano Sen, 36, were reportedly cremated following the service

Innocent: Sunando Sen, 46, a native of Calcutta, India, was shoved onto the subway tracks in front of an oncoming train earlier this week

The Indian immigrant shoved into the path of an oncoming subway train last week by a Muslim-hating madwoman was remembered today as a 'remarkable man,' by friends who mourned him during a wake at a Queens funeral home.

'He had quiet strength,' Lorcan Otway, 57, reportedly said of Sunando Sen, 36, whose disfigured remains lay swaddled in cloth and adorned with flowers in a blue-grey-colored coffin. '(He was) an Indian Gregory Peck.'

Sen, who emigrated to the U.S. 20 years ago, had no family in America, and his parents, who formerly resided in India, have passed away.

But plenty of people mourned his death at Coppola-Migliore funeral home in Flushing, according to The New York Daily News and The New York Post, which both reported on the service.

'I feel like I lost a family member. The neighborhood, the shop, was his family,' Bidyut Sarker, Sen’s boss at the Manhattan print shop where he worked for past 15 years, told The New York Post.

Sarker, who heloped pay for her one-time employee's burial, then added to the paper: 'Customers are coming in and crying.'

'He wasn't making the speeches, but he was quietly doing the work. ... I hope the lesson people will learn is the lesson of peace,' Otway reportedly added

Sen, who friends said had recently opened his own printing shop on Amsterdam Avenue, was remembered as 'a gentleman' and exceptionally smart, The Post reported.

'He didn’t have a hateful bone in his body,' Otway said. 'He approached everything with a calmness. The remarkable man he was should teach us a lesson. I wish people could know the greater loss to the community.'

Sen got a scholarship to New York University and earned a master’s degree in economics, there, and had tried to attain a PhD at Columbia before dropping out because he ran short of funds, friends said.

Meanwhile, new details emerged concerning Erica Menendez, the 31-year-old madwoman who pushed Sen to his death last week before an oncoming 7 train at Queens subway station.

On the run: Menendez was captured on a surveillance camera fleeing from the train station after the incident

Clue: Police obtained grainy surveillance footage from a nearby pizzeria showing a woman said to be wearing a blue, white and gray ski jacket and Nike sneakers running away

On the run: Menendez, captured on surveillance video fleeing the scene, was reportedly muttering to herself

Menendez, who has been charged with murder as a hate crime, reportedly confessed to cops that she killed Sen because 'I've hated Hindus and Muslims since 2001 since they put down the Twin Towers.'

Police sources told The News cops were called by Menendez’ family five times between 2005 and February when she refused to take her meds.

The News cites sources as saying that in one of these instances, Menendez threw a radio at one of the responding officers.

Hunt: NYPD chief spokesman Paul Browne said Menendez fled the scene, pictured, after the shocking attack and officers were searching for her

Investigation: Officers, pictured, quizzed witnesses after the shocking incident

Meanwhile, neighbors told the paper Menendez 'got wacko' when she did not take her medication.

'She started charging at me. She started clawing me in the face,' retired FDNY battalion chief Daniel Conlisk told the News of a bizarre encounter he had with Menendez in 2003 while took out his trash.

Sources told The News Menendez was arrested three times in 2003, twice for assault and once for possession of cocaine. She also punched, scratched and slapped a 28-year-old man she was living with in April 2003, the source added to The News.
