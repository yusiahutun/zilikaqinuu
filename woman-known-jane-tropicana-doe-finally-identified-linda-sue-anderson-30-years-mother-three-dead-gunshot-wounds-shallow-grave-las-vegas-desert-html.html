Woman known only as 'Jane Tropicana Doe' is finally identified as Linda Sue Anderson more than 30 YE

June 2024 Â· 3 minute read

Las Vegas police have identified the remains of a woman found dead in a shallow grave about 30 years ago.

The woman known only as 'Jane Tropicana Doe' has been identified as Linda Sue Anderson, a mother of three who was about 38 years old at the time of her death, according to 8 News Now Investigators.

Anderson, who also used the last name Andrews while she was alive, lived in Las Vegas and had ties to Bakersfield, California.

In the spring of 1993, scientists working near Tropicana Avenue and Durango Drive in the southwest valley about seven miles west of the Las Vegas strip, discovered Anderson's shallow grave.

The grave was covered with a quilt and rocks to hide her decomposing body. The scientists had been canvassing the area for tortoises, authorities said.

Remains  found in 1993 are now identified as Linda Sue Anderson, a 38-year-old mother of three who was never reported missing

Scientists canvassing the southwest valley for tortoises stumbled across Anderson's shallow grave

It was later determined by the Clark County coroner that the woman likely died in mid-to-late 1991 from multiple gunshot wounds, said LVMPH Cold Case Investigator Terri Miller.

Anderson was never reported missing by her family, Miller said. Her children told the investigator she had suffered from mental illness and they believed she'd walked out on them.

Miller said the children now feel 'a tremendous amount of guilt' for not reporting the case.

'They felt that their mom had abandoned them,' she said.

'She was a good mom when she was doing well. They had wonderful memories with her. But when she was doing badly, it was more like the children were taking care of their mother,' she said.

Speaking to the local news, Miller said when Anderson's children reached out to their stepfather to ask where she was, 'he stated that he came home one day and she had left.'

'And it was a plausible story for them based on their history with their mom,' she added. 

The Vegas police worked with Texas-based Othram Labs to confirm 'Jane Tropicana Doe's' identity late last year. The group has previously work with the Metro PD on similar cold-case murders.

The company received the woman's remains in early 2023 and eventually identified Anderson using genetic genealogy.

The case, however, remains unsolved though authorities are hopeful the positive identification may lead to additional information about the killer.

Now, that the ID has been returned, authorities are hoping it will lead to new information about Anderson's killer

Forensic experts believe genetic genealogy could bring justice to a number of similar cases that have long-gone cold. 

It is the hope of many researchers that tech could someday allow authorities to identify a killer's DNA directly from a crime scene instantly, even if the suspect is not in a criminal database. 

In recent years, genetic genealogy has been used to arrest several infamous serial killers, including the Golden State Killer and the 'Grim Sleeper,' after samples from crime scenes were linked to their unsuspecting relatives. 

The growing field also led law enforcement to one of the highest profile arrests in recent memory, that of accused University of Idaho quadruple murderer Bryan Kohberger.
